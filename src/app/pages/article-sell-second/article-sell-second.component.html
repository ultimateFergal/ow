Creating Tax Friendly
Conditions to Sell Your Business

The sale of a business is a big ticket item, the government will want its cut. If you’ve implemented an aggressive “tax minimization” strategy in the past you’ll likely need to add back some items to show the true earning potential to a buyer. At the very least, you’ll need an accountant with auditing experience to organize, document, and verify your business’s tax filings as well as give you a sense how the buyers tax status will affect the sale.

Keep in mind the buyer will also want a tax friendly sale so proposing to defer disproportionate amounts of tax liability may not work with sophisticated buyers. The tax dance can be discordant when sellers do not lead. When done correctly a tax compromise can instead be an important point of confluence for all parties. This is where an experienced CPA can work inclusively between both parties. Alternatively, imagine going at it by yourself or with a timid CPA. If you find yourself having to take time away from the business to find common ground with the buyers accountant, know your deal is going awry. This is not to mention the various regulatory bodies that have a say in how much it should cost to sell a business. At the highest levels there are the SEC and IRS, but depending on your industry there may be other regulators to navigate especially if your business rings any public safety alarms. We’ve worked with the best CPAs to navigate these issues before. If these issues are important to the sale of your business, let us connect you with a CPA who has a track record of successfully overcoming them.

The CPA Manages the Sale Negotiation
While You Focus on the Business

In most cases, a sale brings on critical pressure for the business to perform. An owner who takes the focus away from business operations nearing the time of sale is in essence setting the business up to be sold at a discount. During this critical point, buyers inevitably will try to propose adjustments to the purchase price. Your accountant will counter with not only what is in your best interest but also what might be mutually beneficial from a tax perspective. We entirely expect that the amount a CPA will save the seller in time and taxes will pay the cost of hiring that expert.

Bringing a Buyer Back
to Close the Deal

You may have a good idea what the business should be worth from a cash flow perspective but what about any risks the new buyer will face after you’re gone? The buyer will assign a significant discount to anything that smells like transition risk. So much so they may ultimately find the business uninheritable. An objective CPA can bring the buyer back into the conversation by assigning a measurable value to each of the accompanying risks. Sellers should not underestimate how difficult it can be to view your own business in the worst possible light and then assign a market value to that worst scenario. That is how the buyer will think about risk. Included in the conversation with your CPA will be preparations to negotiate with overly pessimistic buyers. Imagine yourself looking objectively at this sort of buyer and walking away from the sale with confidence in having made the right decision. That sort of confidence can only come from having assigned an objective market value to the downside risk associated with the business.

With a Deal

Either way you are moving forward and should expect the value of your business to increase after having engaged with a sell side CPA.
